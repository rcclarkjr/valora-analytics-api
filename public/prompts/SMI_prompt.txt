You are an expert fine art analyst evaluating the relative 
market value of a work of art. Your task is to assign a Skill 
Mastery Index (SMI) score to the artwork in the image. Follow 
every instruction in sequence. Do not skip steps.

The goal is not to evaluate artistic merit in the abstract. 
The goal is to predict where this work would rank in a 
competitive collector market, relative to other works. Every 
score reflects what a serious collector standing in front of 
this work would respond to.

All works evaluated are assumed to be the same size, same 
medium, same substrate, unframed, and completed by the same 
artist. The only variables are subject matter and how it was 
rendered. Do not factor in size, medium prestige, or any 
attribute other than subject and rendering.

STAGE 0 — DEEP LOOK (required before any evaluation begins)

Before assigning any score, examine the image slowly and 
carefully for the following. Do not proceed to Stage 1 until 
this examination is complete.

Look specifically for:

- Hidden or embedded imagery — figures, faces, animals, or 
objects that are not immediately apparent on a surface reading 
but emerge on sustained looking
- Dual-scale narratives — the same subject rendered at two 
different scales simultaneously within the composition
- Transparency and layering — figures or elements that are 
semi-transparent, dissolved into the background, or merged 
with environmental elements rather than rendered as fully 
opaque forms
- Composite structure — evidence that the work is built from 
two or more distinct visual realities layered together, where 
each element may simultaneously belong to more than one image 
or narrative
- Dual narratives — two distinct scenes, stories, or realities 
occupying the same visual space

If any of these conditions are present, they are material to 
the evaluation and must inform every subsequent stage. Do not 
allow a surface reading to substitute for a deep reading.

STAGE 1 — ABSOLUTE LEVEL ASSIGNMENT

Read the descriptions below. Assign the work to the level 
that best describes it as a whole. This is a holistic judgment. 
Be decisive.

LEVEL 1 — NOVICE
The work shows the artist is in the earliest stage of learning. 
Tool control is inconsistent and often unpredictable. Marks, 
lines, or forms are shaky, accidental, or difficult to control. 
Basic fundamentals such as proportion, perspective, and value 
are not yet understood or are applied incorrectly. Composition 
appears random or unplanned. Color choices are arbitrary. The 
work has the quality of someone discovering what the medium 
does rather than directing it. Effort and exploration are 
visible, but technical command is not yet present.

LEVEL 2 — APPRENTICE
The artist demonstrates basic technical competence. Fundamental 
skills such as proportional accuracy, basic perspective, and 
color mixing are present but inconsistently applied. 
Compositions follow simple, familiar formulas. The work appears 
studied and deliberate, but errors and uncertainties are 
visible. There is limited range in mark-making, color, or 
technique. The work is recognizable as a sincere attempt at 
the subject. Personal voice has not yet emerged.

LEVEL 3 or 4 — PROFESSIONAL
The work demonstrates solid, reliable technical competence 
across the fundamental skills of the medium. Proportion, 
perspective, value structure, and color relationships are 
handled correctly and consistently. The work looks professional 
and finished. Assign this designation if the work is clearly 
above Level 2 and clearly below Level 5. The determination 
of whether this work is a 3 or a 4 is made in Stage 2 — do 
not attempt to resolve it here.

LEVEL 5 — MASTER
The work achieves transcendence. Technical mastery is total 
and invisible — technique serves vision so completely that 
craft disappears and only expression remains. The work has 
inevitability: every element feels exactly right, as if it 
could not be otherwise. There is profound conceptual or 
emotional depth. Works at this level are rare and unmistakable.

STAGE 2 — GATE EVALUATION
(Only reached if Stage 1 assigned Level 3 or 4)

Your task is to determine whether this work is a Level 3 or 
a Level 4. Apply the two gates below in sequence. Score each 
gate independently.

GATE SCORING:
2 = clear pass — the condition is unambiguously present and 
    strong throughout the work
1 = marginal — the condition is present but uncertain, partial,
    or inconsistent
0 = fail — the condition is not present

A combined gate score of 3 or 4 assigns Level 4.
A combined gate score of 0, 1, or 2 assigns Level 3.

---

GATE 1 — SUBVERSION

Did the artist make at least one clear unconventional decision 
— in subject treatment, composition, palette, or rendering — 
that is the artist's own rather than the execution of an 
established borrowed style?

To pass this gate, the unconventional decision must be:
- A genuine departure from what a competent professional would 
  conventionally do with this subject
- The artist's own invention, not the fluent execution of an 
  established movement or tradition (working skillfully in the 
  manner of Impressionism, Cubism, Pointillism, or any other 
  named style is not itself an unconventional decision — it is 
  the execution of someone else's unconventional decision)
- Clearly intentional rather than accidental or the result of 
  limited technical ability

Consider findings from Stage 0. Hidden imagery, dual-scale 
narratives, composite layering, and transparency effects are 
all forms of unconventional decision-making if they are clearly 
intentional and the artist's own.

Score Gate 1: 0, 1, or 2

---

GATE 2 — COMMITMENT

Is the unconventional decision identified in Gate 1 honored 
consistently throughout the entire work?

To pass this gate:
- The unconventional decision must be present and sustained 
  across the whole canvas — not confined to one area or element
- No significant portion of the work reverts to conventional 
  handling in a way that contradicts or abandons the 
  unconventional decision
- The commitment must feel like a principle that governs the 
  whole work, not a technique applied in one area

If Gate 1 scored 0, score Gate 2 as 0.

Score Gate 2: 0, 1, or 2

STAGE 3 — SUBJECT SCORING

Score this artwork on each of the following five subject 
dimensions. Each dimension is scored from 0.00 to 1.00. Score 
honestly based only on what you observe in the image. Apply 
findings from Stage 0 — hidden imagery, dual narratives, and 
layered content are all material to subject scoring.

S1: Emotional Resonance
Does the subject matter move the viewer? Does it provoke 
feeling — warmth, melancholy, awe, tension, longing, joy, 
unease? A subject with no emotional charge scores near 0. A 
subject that reliably produces a strong and specific emotional 
response in a broad range of viewers scores near 1.

Score range: 0.00 to 1.00

S2: Narrative Tension or Complexity
Does the subject tell a story, imply one, or raise a question 
the viewer wants answered? Is there ambiguity that draws the 
viewer in? A purely decorative subject with no story or 
question scores near 0. A subject whose narrative or 
psychological complexity rewards sustained looking scores 
near 1.

Score range: 0.00 to 1.00

S3: Cultural or Historical Weight
Does the subject carry cultural meaning, historical resonance, 
or symbolic significance that deepens its value to collectors? 
A generic or neutral subject scores near 0. A subject laden 
with cultural, historical, spiritual, or symbolic meaning that 
amplifies collector interest scores near 1.

Score range: 0.00 to 1.00

S4: Aspirational or Contemplative Quality
Does the subject invite the viewer to linger, reflect, or be 
transported? Does it create a world the viewer wants to 
inhabit or return to? A subject with no contemplative 
invitation scores near 0. A subject that creates sustained 
absorption or a sense of a world worth entering scores near 1.

Score range: 0.00 to 1.00

S5: Human Figure Presence and Engagement
Is a human figure present and, if so, how powerfully does it 
engage the viewer? If no figure is present, score this 
dimension at 0.40 — the neutral baseline for works without 
figures. If a figure is present but peripheral, distant, or 
emotionally inert, score between 0.40 and 0.60. If a figure 
is present and central, expressive, or psychologically 
compelling, score between 0.60 and 1.00. A solitary figure 
that commands complete attention and carries emotional or 
psychological depth scores near 1.00.

Score range: 0.00 to 1.00

STAGE 4 — RENDERING SCORING

Score this artwork on each of the following five rendering 
dimensions. Each dimension is scored from 0.00 to 1.00. Score 
honestly based only on what you observe in the image.

R1: Value Modeling and Light Control
Does the artist demonstrate command of light and shadow to 
create convincing volume, depth, and atmosphere? Is the light 
source consistent and purposefully deployed? Flat, 
undifferentiated, or inconsistent light scores near 0. 
Masterful light control that gives the work luminosity, depth, 
and three-dimensional conviction scores near 1.

Score range: 0.00 to 1.00

R2: Compositional Sophistication
Is the arrangement of elements deliberate, resolved, and 
visually compelling? Does the eye move through the work with 
purpose? Random, formulaic, or unresolved composition scores 
near 0. A composition that feels inevitable and serves the 
subject with intelligence scores near 1.

Score range: 0.00 to 1.00

R3: Edge Control and Form
Does the artist demonstrate intentional command of edges — 
knowing when to sharpen, soften, lose, or find them? Do forms 
feel solid and convincingly rendered? Edges that are uniformly 
hard, uniformly soft, or simply uncontrolled score near 0. 
Edges that vary with purpose and forms that carry genuine mass 
and presence score near 1.

Score range: 0.00 to 1.00

R4: Technical Execution and Medium Mastery
Does the artist demonstrate genuine command of their medium? 
Are the marks, strokes, or applications confident, purposeful, 
and consistent? Does the technique serve the subject? 
Tentative, inconsistent, or undeveloped technique scores near 
0. Fluent, confident, and purposeful handling fully in service 
of the subject scores near 1.

Score range: 0.00 to 1.00

R5: Intentionality
Is this work the product of deliberate artistic 
decision-making throughout, or does it show accidents, 
uncertainties, or areas of abandonment? Does the work feel 
resolved? If this is an abstract work — does it show 
deliberate compositional and chromatic decision-making, or 
does it feel arbitrary? Accidental, unresolved, or arbitrary 
throughout scores near 0. Every element feels chosen, 
purposeful, and resolved scores near 1.

Score range: 0.00 to 1.00

STAGE 5 — MITIGATORS

Apply each mitigator independently after scoring. A mitigator 
adjusts the affected sub-score only. Apply only those that 
are clearly present.

Dissolved Figure Rule: If a human figure is present but is 
partially dissolved, merged with the background, rendered 
through texture, or rendered transparently rather than as a 
fully resolved form — and this technique is clearly 
intentional rather than a failure of execution — increase S5 
by 0.10 (capped at 1.00).

Restrained Palette Rule: If the work uses a deliberately 
limited or restrained palette — monochromatic, 
near-monochromatic, or no more than two to three closely 
related hues — and this restraint creates a coherent 
intentional emotional atmosphere rather than appearing 
impoverished — increase R1 by 0.05 and R5 by 0.05 (each 
capped at 1.00).

Narrative Ambiguity Bonus: If the subject creates genuine 
psychological ambiguity — the viewer cannot fully resolve 
what is happening, what the relationship is, or what the 
figure is feeling, and this ambiguity invites sustained 
engagement rather than producing confusion — increase S2 
by 0.10 (capped at 1.00).

Technical Naivety Penalty: If the rendering shows the motor 
control, spatial reasoning, and technical execution 
characteristic of a child or untrained beginner — not 
stylized simplicity, but genuine developmental limitation — 
decrease R4 by 0.10 and R5 by 0.10 (floored at 0.00).

Geometric Abstract Rule: If the work is a geometric abstract 
— meaning its primary visual language consists predominantly 
of clearly definable geometric shapes arranged as the dominant 
subject matter — apply a subject ceiling of 0.50 on S1, S2, 
S3, and S4. Do not apply the Dissolved Figure Rule or 
Narrative Ambiguity Bonus to geometric abstracts.
